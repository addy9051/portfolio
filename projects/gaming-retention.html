Business Context
Who the client is, what the competitive landscape looks like, and why churn was the right problem to solve
BA

GamerX Interactive Studios is a mid-size gaming publisher with 40,000+ active players across multiple genres — Strategy, Action, Sports, RPG, and Simulation. Revenue flows through in-app purchases (primary) and advertising (secondary), with players segmented from casual free-to-play users through to high-spend "Whale" players generating $200–500 in lifetime value each.

The core economics of gaming create a structural churn problem: acquiring a new player costs 5–10× more than retaining an existing one. A churned Whale is catastrophically expensive. Yet most gaming companies run uniform retention campaigns — the same 10% discount goes to a Whale and a day-one casual player, wasting budget on players who were never going to churn while under-investing in those who were.

Player base
40,000+ active players across 5 game genres and 4 global regions (North America, Europe, Asia, Other)
Revenue model
In-app purchases (primary) + advertising (secondary). Player LTV ranges $5–500 depending on engagement tier
Churn rate (baseline)
~25–30% of players showing EngagementLevel = Low or DaysSinceLastLogin > 14 at any given time
Revenue at risk
$1.2–1.8M annually in projected LTV from churning players — unquantified before this project

Problem Statement
What was broken and what it was costing the business
BA DA

The marketing team ran one global re-engagement campaign: a 10% in-app purchase discount. It went to every player flagged as at-risk — Whales, casual players, and everyone in between — with no differentiation by value, behavior, or predicted trajectory. High-LTV players received the same message as players who had never spent a dollar.

Problem 1 — No churn prediction
The only churn signal was DaysSinceLastLogin > 14 — a reactive, lagging indicator. By the time a player was flagged, they had already mentally disengaged. There was no forward-looking model.
Problem 2 — No segmentation
All 40K players were treated identically regardless of LTV, play style, or engagement trajectory. The same $0.25 push notification went to a Whale worth $500 LTV and a new player worth $5.
Problem 3 — No ROI framework
Campaign spend was a fixed budget with no measurement of revenue recovered per dollar invested. The team had no way to know whether running the campaign was better than doing nothing.
Business cost
$1.2–1.8M in annual LTV walking out the door, campaign budget wasted on uniform interventions, and no feedback loop to improve over time.

Root Cause Analysis
Why the existing approach structurally couldn't work — and what the real fix required
BA DA
🔍
Signal: static averages hide declining players
A player averaging 40 minutes per session could be stable at 40 min all year, or declining from 70 min in week 1 to 10 min in week 8. Both look identical in the raw feature data. The existing login-days metric captured absence after churn had already occurred — not the behavioral decline that precedes it.
🧩
MECE decomposition — three distinct problems
Separating the problem cleanly revealed three workstreams that each needed independent solutions: (1) Identification — which players will churn in the next 14 days, requiring a predictive model with leading indicators. (2) Valuation — what is each player's LTV and revenue at risk, requiring an economic model. (3) Intervention — what action maximises expected ROI per player, requiring an expected-value decision framework per persona.
🎯
Root cause: treating players as a homogeneous population
The fundamental error was applying one model, one metric, and one campaign to a player base with radically different economics and behaviors. The fix required: (a) segmentation that reflects actual behavioral differences, (b) a predictive model that uses cross-temporal behavioral signals — not just current-state attributes, and (c) a campaign framework where intervention type and spend are determined by expected value, not gut instinct.

RFM feature engineering (gaming adaptation)

Standard RFM (Recency-Frequency-Monetary) is adapted to gaming, where "Recency" means session engagement depth, not days since purchase.

Engagement velocity — the key technical innovation

8-week rolling session logs are synthesised per player and used to compute temporal trend features. These proved to be the highest-impact addition: +0.03–0.04 AUC improvement over the static-feature model alone. A player whose sessions are declining from 70 → 10 min/week looks identical in snapshot data to one stable at 40 min — velocity features break that ambiguity.

FeatureInterpretationChurn red flag
Duration_SlopeLinear regression slope over 8 weeks (min/week change)< −2.0 min/week
WoW_Change_PctMean week-over-week % change in session duration< −5% per week
Acceleration2nd derivative — is the decline itself accelerating?Negative acceleration
Last_vs_FirstWeek-8 duration ÷ Week-1 duration< 0.5 (halved)
Session_Count_SlopeLinear trend of sessions per week over 8 weeks< −0.3/week
Duration_CVCoefficient of variation — erratic = disengagedHigh CV
Min_Max_RatioMin week ÷ Max week session durationLow ratio = high volatility
Duration_R²How consistent is the linear trend?High R² + negative slope = confirmed decline

Revenue-weighted training — aligning the model's loss function to business cost

Standard binary classification treats every misclassification equally — a false negative on a $5 Newbie costs the same as a false negative on a $500 Whale. That's wrong. XGBoost's sample_weight parameter is set to InGamePurchases during training, so the model learns to prioritise recall on high-revenue players. The revenue-weighted AUC (0.85–0.88) is meaningfully higher than the unweighted AUC (0.82–0.85) — confirming the business-aligned model is better at protecting the players that matter most.

Implementation details

FastAPI serving + telemetry
X-API-Key header required on all endpoints. POST /predict returns churn probability + persona label per player. Each request logged as telemetry (player_id, churn_prob, persona, timestamp) — output feeds back into the campaign performance loop for model drift monitoring.
Model serialization + versioning
category_mappings.pkl (StringIndexer) persisted alongside XGBoost — maps Genre, Region, and EngagementLevel to integer codes at training time and reloaded at inference to prevent training–serving skew. Artifacts saved with YYYYMMDD_HHMMSS timestamp + model_latest alias for zero-downtime rollback. MODEL_PATH and MAPPINGS_PATH env vars control which version the API loads without code changes.

Reflections & What I'd Do Differently
Honest technical retrospective — the part most portfolios skip
DS BA
✓ What worked well
Revenue-weighting the XGBoost training was the single best decision in the project. It moved the model's optimization target from "predict all churners equally" to "protect high-LTV players first" — and the revenue-weighted AUC confirmed it actually worked. This is the kind of business-ML alignment that separates projects that look good from projects that deliver value.
The expected-value intervention trigger (EV = churn_prob × LTV − campaign_cost > 2.0) was the right framework. It meant campaigns only fired when ROI was mathematically justified — preventing the anti-pattern of blasting every "at-risk" player with a discount.
Validating velocity features empirically rather than assuming they'd help — by running the AUC comparison before and after adding them — gave a concrete, defensible justification for the additional complexity. The +0.04 AUC is not assumed; it's measured.
↺ What I'd do differently
The velocity features are simulated from the base dataset rather than derived from actual session event logs. In production I'd replace the simulation with a proper event streaming pipeline (Kafka → Spark Structured Streaming → feature store), which would make the velocity features real and enable online prediction at the moment of session end — not just in a daily batch job.
I used a global 75% threshold to export to CRM. A better design would be persona-aware thresholds — a 60% Whale is worth intervening on immediately, while a 75% Newbie might not justify the campaign cost. I'd fold the EV calculation directly into the CRM export logic rather than keeping it separate.
The A/B test simulation validates the campaign framework statistically (p < 0.05 on simulated data), but real deployment needs an online controlled experiment with randomised holdout groups per persona, tracked through an experiment platform. I'd wire the FastAPI telemetry logs directly into an experiment tracking dashboard from day one.
