Business Context
What the company does, the market condition, and why this mattered
BA

A Global Travel Agency manages end-to-end corporate travel programmes for approximately 5,000 business accounts — ranging from SMEs to multinationals. Revenue flows from subscription management fees, per-transaction commissions on flights, hotels, and ground transport, and ancillary service bundles.

The post-pandemic rebound created a paradox: corporate travel volumes were recovering fast, but account churn was accelerating in parallel. Procurement teams were scrutinising every vendor contract, and travel managers were consolidating providers. The agency needed to shift from a reactive service model to a proactive commercial one.

Problem Statement
What was broken, and the cost of leaving it unsolved
BA DA

The commercial team had no forward-looking signal: they could not tell which accounts were worth retaining, which were likely to leave, or which services each account was most likely to adopt next — so every intervention was reactive and every cross-sell conversation was a cold call.

Stakeholder pain
Account managers spending time on at-risk accounts only after receiving cancellation notices — too late to intervene effectively.
Business cost
Revenue leakage from two sources: undetected churn eroding the base, and unguided cross-sell leaving wallet share on the table.

Root Cause Analysis
How I diagnosed the problem before writing a single line of model code
BA DA
🔍
Signal identification
Exploratory analysis revealed that account spend frequency — not total spend — was the leading indicator of disengagement. Accounts that reduced booking cadence by >30% over a rolling 90-day window had a 3× higher cancellation rate in the following quarter. This signal existed in the transaction data but was never surfaced.
🧩
MECE decomposition of the revenue problem
Revenue per account = (Baseline spend × Retention probability) + (Cross-sell revenue × Adoption probability). The agency was managing none of these four components analytically. Each required a separate modelling objective: CLV regression for baseline, survival modelling for retention, clustering for segment strategy, and classification for adoption.
🎯
Root cause pinpointed
No integrated ML pipeline existed to convert raw transaction signals into commercial predictions. The data sat in a Snowflake warehouse but had never been modelled. The fix was not a reporting dashboard — it was a connected inference system that could score every account on four dimensions and route the output to the right stakeholder.

Why these models?

Objective Chosen model Why not the alternative
12-month revenue prediction (CLV) LightGBM Regressor XGBoost was evaluated alongside LightGBM — both are gradient-boosted tree ensembles. LightGBM was selected for its GPU-accelerated training speed, intrinsic null handling (no separate imputation step), and sharper SHAP feature-importance mapping. Final: R²=0.61, MAE=$14.8K on held-out test set.
Churn risk scoring Cox Proportional Hazards Binary classifiers require a fixed time horizon and discard when-to-churn information. Cox PH gives a hazard curve per account, which is more actionable for CSMs who need lead time.
Customer segmentation UMAP + HDBSCAN K-Means assumes spherical clusters and requires k upfront. HDBSCAN finds density-based clusters of arbitrary shape and naturally handles noise accounts as outliers.
Product recommendation XGBoost Classifier (per product) Collaborative filtering requires dense interaction matrices — not available for B2B with sparse purchase history. Separate binary classifiers per product give interpretable feature importances per service.

DSPy agentic outreach
OutreachSignature(dspy.Signature) with 8 ML-derived input fields (CLV score, churn hazard, top cross-sell product, segment, spend data). Uses dspy.ChainOfThought to ground reasoning in predictions before generating email subject + body. max_tokens=2048 via Azure OpenAI GPT-4o.
n8n automation + monitoring
Hourly schedule trigger → Snowflake query for high-risk + high-CLV accounts → POST /generate/outreach → Slack alert to account manager.

Prometheus scrapes /metrics every 15 s via prometheus_fastapi_instrumentator; Grafana dashboard on port 3000 for API drift monitoring.

Key Findings & Insights
The analytical output — what the data actually showed
DA DS
63%
Revenue from top 20% of accounts
Classic Pareto held: the highest CLV tier (1,000 accounts) generated nearly two-thirds of total agency revenue. Loss of even 5% of this tier would outweigh gains from the entire long tail.
8–12w
Churn signal lead time
The Cox model identified at-risk accounts 8–12 weeks before cancellation based on declining booking frequency and reduced service diversity — a window large enough for proactive CSM outreach.
4
Distinct customer segments
HDBSCAN found four stable clusters: Champions (high CLV, high engagement), At-Risk High-Value (high CLV, declining activity), Growth Potential (low CLV, rising trajectory), and Dormant (low spend, infrequent bookings).
Hotel + Ground
Highest-probability cross-sell bundle
Accounts already purchasing flight management showed 73% adoption probability for hotel booking services, and 68% for ground transport — the two highest-confidence cross-sell recommendations across all product pairs.

Model output charts

90-day spend and booking count dominate CLV predictions. Recency features (total_spend_90d, booking_count_90d) carry more predictive weight than tenure — an account that books frequently in the last quarter is a stronger CLV signal than one with years of low-frequency history.
High-tier accounts churn faster than low-tier ones — the counterintuitive finding. High-spend accounts have more leverage in vendor consolidation decisions and exit earlier when dissatisfied. This reversed the assumption that high-value accounts were the safest, and refocused retention effort on them specifically.
At-Risk accounts (red, left panel) cluster tightly and spatially separate from the main body. The right panel overlays CLV heat — the densest high-CLV region overlaps exactly with the At-Risk cluster, confirming that this is the highest-value cohort also facing the greatest churn hazard. The business intervention target is unambiguous.

Reflections & What I'd Do Differently
Honest assessment — the section most junior portfolios omit
BA DS
✓ What worked well
Choosing Cox Proportional Hazards over a binary churn classifier was the right call — the hazard curve per account gave CSMs a "how soon" signal, not just "yes/no", which is far more actionable in practice.
The dbt medallion architecture (Bronze → Silver → Gold) made data lineage auditable and the feature store reusable across all four models — this saved significant rework when I iterated on features.
Separating the modelling problem into four distinct objectives (CLV, churn, segmentation, cross-sell) rather than building one monolithic model made each component testable and explainable independently.
↺ What I'd do differently
I'd integrate a real CRM (Salesforce or HubSpot) into the outreach module from the start. The DSPy + GPT-4o pipeline generates strong personalised emails, but without CRM write-back it remains a demo rather than a production workflow.
I'd add macroeconomic signals as external features — corporate travel demand correlates with fuel prices, business confidence indices, and regional travel policy. These would likely improve CLV R² meaningfully.
I'd build an online learning loop so the models update incrementally as new booking data arrives, rather than requiring a full retrain cycle. MLflow handles versioning, but the trigger logic for retraining is still manual.
