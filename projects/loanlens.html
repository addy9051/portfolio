Key Terms
Plain-language definitions for the terms used throughout this page
AI-analysis "completion"
A user who reaches ai_analysis_completed — the LLM finished analysing their bank statement and they didn't abandon during the wait. The metric the whole project optimises.
Drop-off
The share of users who start the AI analysis but never complete it. The ~33% headline is this figure for the AI-analysis step.
LLM latency
How long the LLM takes to analyse a bank statement. The longer the wait, the more users abandon — the root cause of the drop-off.
Fallback router
The fix: the gateway calls the primary model with a 4-second budget; if it's too slow, it falls back to a faster, cheaper model.
Converted user / funded loan
A user who reaches loan_accepted — an actual funded loan. "Cost per converted user" = total LLM spend ÷ funded loans.
pp (percentage points)
The absolute gap between two rates. +9.2pp means completion rose from ~66.6% to ~75.8% — not a 9.2% relative change.
2×2 factorial experiment
An A/B/C/D test crossing two fixes (progress bar × fallback router) so you measure each effect and their interaction in one experiment.
Interaction (sub-additive)
When two fixes together deliver less than the sum of their individual lifts (they overlap). Measuring it is the whole point of a 2×2.
Non-inferiority guardrail (NIM)
A one-sided test that a safety metric (e.g. default rate) is not meaningfully worse than control, within a pre-registered margin (the NIM). It can fail — that's the point.
Confounder / back-door adjustment
A variable (here requested_amount) that affects both latency and completion, biasing the naïve estimate. Adjusting for it recovers the true effect.
Prime vs Subprime
Credit-quality bands used as a socioeconomic proxy for the fairness check: credit_score ≥ 670 (prime) vs < 670 (subprime).
CUSUM drift detection
A production monitor that raises an alarm when a metric (e.g. latency) drifts upward — used to catch a model regression quickly.
Net margin per loan
The profit the platform keeps per funded loan after costs (funding, expected defaults, servicing, acquisition) — not the interest rate, which is gross revenue upstream of it.

Business Context
The platform, the funnel, and why a single step mattered so much
BA

An online micro-loan platform underwrites small consumer loans by running an applicant's bank statement through an LLM that summarises income, outflows, and risk signals before an offer is made. Revenue comes from the margin on funded loans, so every applicant who starts but never finishes the AI analysis is lost acquisition spend with nothing to show for it.

The funnel is six steps: sign_up → bank_link → ai_analysis_initiated → ai_analysis_completed → loan_terms_offered → loan_accepted. Product flagged that roughly a third of users vanish between analysis initiated and completed — the single largest leak in the funnel, and the one nobody could explain.

Problem Statement
What was broken, and what it cost to leave it alone
BADA

The AI-analysis step lost ~33% of users, but the team had no idea why — was it a UX problem, a model-quality problem, or an infrastructure problem? Without a diagnosis, every proposed fix was a guess, and any "fix" risked speeding things up at the expense of credit quality.

Ambiguity
No one could attribute the drop to a cause, so prioritisation stalled — UX, ML, and infra each blamed the others.
The trap
Any speed fix could silently raise the loan-default rate — unacceptable in a regulated lending context. The fix needed a hard credit-quality guardrail.

Root Cause Analysis
Diagnosing the drop honestly — including the confounder that biases the naïve answer
DADS
🔍
Latency cohorts surface the signal
Bucketing AI-analysis sessions by LLM latency, completion craters monotonically: 86.4% (<3s) → 78.9% (3–5s) → 63.1% (5–8s) → 43.3% (>8s). The >8s cohort completes at roughly half the rate of the fast cohort. The drop-off is latency-driven, not random impatience.
⚠
…but the naïve estimate is confounded
A naïve "latency → completion" regression is biased: requested_amount is a common cause. Bigger loans → richer bank statements → more tokens → higher latency, and bigger asks → more committed applicants → higher completion. So high-latency requests come from more-committed users, masking latency's true harm.
🎯
Back-door adjustment recovers the true effect
Adjusting for requested_amount (the back-door criterion) moves the latency coefficient from −0.264 (naïve) → −0.298 (+12.8% stronger) — confirming the naïve number understates the damage. The synthetic data was deliberately built with this confounder so the adjustment does real work rather than recovering a baked-in number.

The fix — a latency-fallback router. The gateway calls the primary model (llama3.2) with a hard 4-second budget; if it blows the budget it falls back to a fast model (llama3.2:1b). Every call is traced in Langfuse with model, tokens, latency, and a notional cost, grouped by a per-analysis session_id so the timed-out primary and the fallback land in one trace.

Key Findings & Results
The fix works — and it's validated rigorously, not just asserted
DADS
+9.2pp
The 2×2 factorial fix
Progress bar +3.3pp, fallback router +8.5pp, both +9.2pp completion — with a significant sub-additive interaction (the two fixes share some lift). The interaction is exactly why a 2×2 beats two separate A/Bs.
4.8×
The router cuts cost AND lifts completion
LLM cost per converted user: primary $0.036 vs fast $0.0076. The fast fallback is ~4.8× cheaper per conversion and faster (so it abandons less) — the core LLM-ops result.
+0.64pp
Default-rate guardrail holds
One-sided, fixed-horizon non-inferiority test: the 95% upper bound on the default-rate increase is +0.64pp, below the pre-registered 1.0pp margin → non-inferior. Speed didn't silently degrade credit quality.
+1.25pp
Fair-lending guardrail holds
A second non-inferiority test checks the router doesn't widen the prime−subprime completion gap. 95% UB +1.25pp < 2.0pp margin → non-inferior. Falsifiable: an injected disparity exists, so this can fail.

The ship decision

The recommendation gates on three conditions at once: completion improves and the default-rate guardrail holds and the fairness guardrail holds. All three pass → SHIP. Supporting rigor — Sample-Ratio-Mismatch χ² (fires on an injected bad split), an A/A simulation (false-positive rate ≈ 5%, uniform p-values), Benjamini-Hochberg multiple-testing correction, and a CUSUM monitor that catches an injected latency regression in 1 day — all run in the analysis layer and surface on the dashboard.

Why ship both? (the PM's challenge)

Fair pushback: "a progress bar is a 2-day front-end change with zero underwriting risk — why take on the engineering debt of an LLM router?" The honest answer: ship the progress bar first — but it is not enough on its own.

InterventionLiftWhat it actually fixesRisk / ceiling
Progress bar (UX)+3.3ppPerceived wait — users tolerate uncertainty betterDoesn't reduce actual latency; the >8s cohort still completes at only 43.3%
Fallback router (MLOps)+8.5ppActual latency on slow sessions + 4.8× lower cost / conversionNeeds router ops + a model-quality guardrail
Both (sub-additive)+9.2ppComplementary — UX for mid-range waits, router for the tailInteraction −1.5pp: overlapping, not redundant

The progress bar recovers only ~⅓ of the lost cohort and cannot move the >8s tail — the users actually driving the drop-off. They need real speed, not animation; and the 4.8× cost saving is a second-order win a progress bar can't deliver. Verdict: ship the progress bar for the quick, zero-risk win, and the router because it's the only thing that fixes the tail.

Is the fast model safe, not just fast? — the real-model quality gate

Every result above runs on synthetic data with injected effects — honest for testing methods, but silent on how the real llama3.2:1b actually reasons about a bank statement. So the project's one piece of real-model evidence: both Ollama models run a 14-case underwriting benchmark (edge cases included — thin file, income mismatch, gig volatility, loan stacking), graded deterministically (every cited dollar figure verified against the input facts) and then by an independent GPT-4o judge (GitHub Models' free tier) for fact accuracy and hallucination. The gate is deliberately strict, because it governs autonomous approval.

Real-model quality (14 cases)Primary (3B)Fast (1B)Auto-approve bar
Judge fact accuracy74%70%≥ 90% ✗
Hallucination rate64%86%≤ 5% ✗
Recommendation defensible71%43%—
Verdict safe-side (≥ as conservative)86%79%—

The gate failed — and that is the finding. The judge's flags are real, not noise: on a clean-approve case the 1B invented an "existing $400/mo loan" and a "6-year account age" that were never in the facts; elsewhere it hallucinated a "$3,000 credit limit" and an interest rate. Even the 3B hallucinates on ~64% of cases. Neither local model is underwriting-grade.

So the honest boundary: the router makes the analysis step faster and ~4.8× cheaper — a latency and funnel win — not a licence to hand the credit decision to a 1B. The approve/decline call stays gated behind human review and the model's own abstention rule (confidence < 0.45 → no auto-offer). This is a parallel gate, never folded into the synthetic ship decision: two different evidence bases must not be laundered into one green checkmark. A gate that can fail, and did — which is exactly why it's worth having.

Note on metrics — every assumption, in one place. This is a portfolio pilot on synthetic data: the key mechanisms are deliberately injected so each method can be validated against a known ground truth, and the dollar figures use clearly-labeled assumptions rather than real disclosures.

Data & injected ground truth
  • 20,000 synthetic users, fixed random seed; generated with Faker + numpy.
  • Latency is the dominant, causal driver of AI-step abandonment, with secondary device-type and irreducible-noise effects.
  • requested_amount is an injected confounder: bigger loans → more tokens → higher latency, and → more-committed applicants → higher completion.
  • credit_score ~ Beta(5,2) scaled to 300–850 (a realistic left-skewed, FICO-like shape); base loan-default rate ≈ 8%.
  • An injected fairness disparity: the fast fallback model gives subprime applicants ~0.6pp less completion benefit than prime ones — so the fairness guardrail can fail.
LLM & routing
  • Primary model llama3.2 (slower), fast fallback llama3.2:1b; fallback latency threshold = 4 s.
  • Local Ollama is free, so per-call cost uses a notional cloud-equivalent token price (mirroring the generator) purely to demonstrate cost capture.
Experiment & guardrails
  • Fresh randomised 2×2 population, 50,000 users/arm; baseline completion ≈ 66.6% (grounded in the observed funnel).
  • Injected effects: progress bar +3pp, fallback +8pp, with a −1.5pp sub-additive interaction. The lift is measured in the experiment, then projected onto the observed funnel.
  • Non-inferiority margins (pre-registered): default rate 1.0pp, fairness gap 2.0pp; α = 0.05, one-sided, fixed-horizon.
  • credit_score < 670 used as the subprime / socioeconomic proxy (no protected-class data in the synthetic set).
Unit economics (labeled assumptions)
  • Average loan $2,300 (≈ the mean requested_amount in the data).
  • 12% net contribution per funded loan ≈ $276 — an assumed take-rate after funding cost, expected defaults, servicing & acquisition; not the interest rate (which is gross revenue).
  • CAC $1,450 (blueprint SMB-fintech benchmark); funded-per-completion ≈ 0.61 (from the funnel).
  • Annualised by ×365/120 (~3×), since the cohort spans ~120 days; revenue is framed as margin on incremental funded loans, not loan principal.

Reflections & What I'd Do Differently
The honest section most portfolios skip
DS
✓ What worked well
Injecting a real confounder so the back-door adjustment moves the estimate (−0.264 → −0.298) — the causal section demonstrates method value rather than recovering a number I planted.
Making the fairness guardrail falsifiable: an audit caught an earlier version that grouped by the wrong flag and was null-by-construction; rebuilding it as a real non-inferiority test on an injected disparity made it defensible.
Keeping the guardrails fixed-horizon while the primary metric can run sequential — and writing down why in an ADR — reads like production experimentation, not a notebook.
↺ What I'd do differently
Add a sequential mSPRT on the primary completion metric to show always-valid early stopping alongside the fixed-horizon guardrails.
Local Ollama is CPU-bound and slow, so the live gateway proves the mechanism on a few cases while scale analysis uses synthetic traces — a real deployment would fine-tune a small model or buy provisioned throughput instead.
Wire the fairness audit to true protected-class attributes (with consented data + a maturation window on defaults) rather than a credit-score proxy.
