Business Context
What the organisation does, the operating environment, and why this mattered
BA

GlobalCorp Financial Services is a large enterprise with approximately 3,900 employees across 8 departments — Engineering, Sales, Operations, Finance, HR, IT, Legal, and Marketing. The company reimburses employee expenses across 8 categories (Travel, Meals, Software, Equipment, Communication, Entertainment, Supplies, Training), processing $22.32M+ in annual claims via three payment channels: direct deposit, corporate card, and check.

A 50-strong approver pool (department managers) reviews submissions before payment. The internal audit team is responsible for policy enforcement, but with 100K+ annual claims and no automated signal layer, they relied on random sampling and retrospective reviews — a process that structurally missed behavioral fraud patterns invisible at the individual transaction level.

Problem Statement
What was broken, and the cost of leaving it unsolved
BA DA

The internal audit team could see individual claims but had no way to connect them — so expense splitting across three same-day submissions, collusion between an employee and a single approver, and duplicate resubmissions after rejection were completely invisible to the existing review process.

Stakeholder pain
Audit reviews were retrospective and sampled, missing behavioral fraud that only becomes visible across multiple claims or over time — by the time it was caught, money had already left the business.
Business cost
25% non-compliance rate, $3.21M in anomalous spend, and an estimated $12.22M in savings unrealised due to above-benchmark category spending — all unquantified before this project.

Root Cause Analysis
Diagnosing why the audit process failed before designing the solution
BA DA
🔍
Signal identification
EDA revealed that 25% of claims were non-compliant, yet 85% were approved — meaning the approval process was not enforcing policy. The compliance rate was uniform across departments in the audit narrative, masking that Engineering's compliance was as low as 51% in some months. Single-transaction audits simply could not see this.
🧩
MECE decomposition of the fraud and leakage problem
Three separate problems required three separate solutions: (1) Fraud — behavioral signals spanning multiple claims or employees, requiring a cross-claim signal engine. (2) Compliance — policy violation rates by department and category, requiring a structured rule-checking layer. (3) Cost leakage — above-benchmark category spend requiring a statistical benchmarking framework. No existing system addressed any of the three.
🎯
Root cause pinpointed
The data existed — 100K+ records in a structured schema — but had never been connected into a behavioral signal layer. The fix required a pipeline that could (a) compute cross-claim behavioral signals per employee, (b) score and rank every claim against a composite fraud model, and (c) surface findings to audit leadership in a form they could act on without writing SQL.

The 21-signal fraud detection engine

What the data actually showed across fraud, compliance, and cost

Anomalous claims flagged
The Z-score method identified 1,143 claims across the portfolio as statistical outliers (avg Z-score 4.8), totalling $3.21M in anomalous spend. Engineering and Finance had the highest anomaly counts by department.
9,479
Rejected claims — not random
Rejection rate was not uniform: certain department × category combinations showed rejection rates 3–4× the company average. Missing receipt was the single strongest predictor of rejection, with a statistically significant odds ratio from chi-squared analysis.

Engineering was both the top-spending department ($8.99M total, $10.71K per employee) and the lowest compliance scorer. The AI forensic agent classified it as "High Priority Critical" when a compliance threshold of 85% was applied.

Reflections & What I'd Do Differently
Honest assessment — the section most junior portfolios omit
BA DS
✓ What worked well
The three-tier signal architecture (transaction → behavioural → approver) was the right design. By separating concerns, each group was independently testable, and the approver-level signals caught collusion patterns that neither transaction nor behavioural analysis alone could surface.
Running all four anomaly methods in parallel and comparing precision/recall against the ground-truth is_fraud label gave the audit team a concrete basis for choosing which method to operationalise — not just a black-box recommendation.
The deadlock-free AI agent design — separate connection pools for the LangGraph checkpointer and the forensic query tools — was a non-obvious but critical architectural detail that would have caused silent failures in production without it.
↺ What I'd do differently
I'd replace the rule-based fraud engine with a hybrid approach: keep the 21 signals as interpretable features, but train a gradient-boosted classifier on top of them rather than using a fixed weighted sum. The weights were manually calibrated — a learned model would optimise them from labelled data.
The Power BI report connects to PostgreSQL via DirectQuery. For a production deployment with 100K+ records, I'd pre-aggregate into a dedicated BI semantic layer (dbt + Snowflake or BigQuery) to reduce query latency and decouple the BI layer from the operational database.
The AI agent currently has read-only forensic tools. I'd add write-back actions — flagging a claim for human review, triggering a Jira ticket for the audit team, or updating a case management system — to close the loop from insight to action.
